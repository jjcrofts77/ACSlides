The two primary integral theorems in three dimensions are Gauss's Divergence Theorem and Stokes' Theorem. These theorems relate integrals over a region to integrals over its boundary, generalising the one-dimensional Fundamental Theorem of Vector Calculus.

In this lecture we consider the Divergence Theorem, which incidentally we have already seen the 2-D version of!

Recall, the divergence form of Green's Theorem:

\[ \color{red}{\boxed{\color{white}{ \oint_C\mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,\mathrm{d}s = \iint_\mathcal{R}\left(\frac{\partial F_1}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial F_2}{\partial y}\right)\,\mathrm{d}x\mathrm{d}y = \iint\nabla\cdot F\,\mathrm{d}x\mathrm{d}y}}} \]

This equation states that the outward flux across a simple curve can be computed by integrating the divergence of the field over the region enclosed by the curve

Gauss generalised this result to three dimensions as we shall see ...

Theorem 9.1 (Gauss's Divergence Theorem)

The flux of a vector field

\[ \mathbf{F} = M(x,y,z)\mathbf{i}+N(x,y,z)\mathbf{j}+P(x,y,z)\mathbf{k} \]

across a closed oriented surface $S$ in the direction of the surface's outward unit normal field $\mathbf{n}$ equals the integral of $\displaystyle \nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}$ over the region $\mathcal{D}$ enclosed by the surface:

\[ \iint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,\mathrm{d}\sigma = \iiint_\mathcal{D} \nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}\,\mathrm{d}V \]

Note:

  1. If the vector field is divergence free on $\mathcal{D}$ then the RHS vanishes
  2. The most common use of the divergence theorem is to calculate nasty surface integrals by turning them into volume integrals

Example 9.1

Find the flux of

\[ \mathbf{F} = xy\mathbf{i}+yz\mathbf{j}+xz\mathbf{k} \]

outward through the surface of the cube cut from the first octant by the planes $x=1$, $y=1$ and $z=1$

Solution

Instead of calculating the flux as a sum of six seperate integrals, one for each face of the cube, we can calculate the flux by integrating the divergence

\[ \nabla\cdot\mathbf{F} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(xy\right)+ \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(yz\right)+ \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(xz\right) = y+z+x \]

over the cube's interior:

\[ \begin{align*} \mathrm{Flux} &=\iint_{S}\mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,\mathrm{d}\sigma = \iiint_\mathcal{D}\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}\,\mathrm{d}V\\ &=\int_0^1\int_0^1\int_0^1 \left(x+y+z\right)\,\mathrm{d}x\mathrm{d}y\mathrm{d}z \end{align*} \]

Example 9.1 (ctd)

\[ \begin{align*} \int_0^1\int_0^1\int_0^1 \left(x+y+z\right)\,\mathrm{d}x\mathrm{d}y\mathrm{d}z &= \int_0^1\int_0^1\bigg[\frac{1}{2}x^2+yx+zx\bigg]_0^1\,\mathrm{d}y\mathrm{d}z\\ &=\int_0^1\int_0^1\left(\frac{1}{2}+y+z\right)\,\mathrm{d}y\mathrm{d}z \\ &= \int_0^1\bigg[\frac{1}{2}y+\frac{1}{2}y^2+zy\bigg]_0^1\,\mathrm{d}z\\ &= \int_0^1 \left(1+z\right)\,\mathrm{d}z = \bigg[z+\frac{1}{2}z^2\bigg]_0^1 = \color{red}{\boxed{\color{white}{\frac{3}{2}}}} \end{align*} \]

This is considerably less work than computing six surface intergals!

Example 9.2

Use the Divergence Theorem to find the outward normal flux of

\[ \mathbf{F} = x^2\mathbf{i}-2xy\mathbf{j}+3xz\mathbf{k} \]

across the boundary of the region $\mathcal{D}$ cut from the first octant by the sphere

\[ x^2+y^2+z^2\leq 4 \]

Solution

We can calculate the flux by integrating the divergence

\[ \nabla\cdot\mathbf{F} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(x^2\right)+ \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\left(-2xy\right)+ \frac{\partial}{\partial z}\left(3xz\right) = 2x-2x+3x = 3x \]

over the interior region, i.e.

\[ \iint_S\mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,\mathrm{d}\sigma = \iiint_\mathcal{D} \nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}\,\mathrm{d}V = \iiint_\mathcal{D} 3x\,\mathrm{d}x\mathrm{d}y\mathrm{d}z \]

Since the region $\mathcal{D}$ is a spherical quadrant this suggests changing to spherical coordinates ...

Example 9.2 (ctd)

In spherical coordinates the region is defined as

\[ r^2\leq 4,\quad \theta\in[0, \pi/2], \quad \phi\in[0, \pi/2] \]

Recall that

\[ \Phi(r,\theta,\phi) = \begin{pmatrix}x(r,\theta, \phi)\\y(r,\theta, \phi)\\z(r,\theta, \phi)\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}r\sin(\phi)\cos(\theta)\\r\sin(\phi)\sin(\theta)\\r\cos(\phi)\end{pmatrix} \]

So that the integral becomes

\[ \begin{align*} \iint_S\mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,\mathrm{d}\sigma &= \iiint_\mathcal{D} \nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}\,\mathrm{d}V \\ &= \iiint_\mathcal{D} 3x\,\mathrm{d}x\mathrm{d}y\mathrm{d}z\\ &= \int_0^{\pi/2}\int_0^{\pi/2}\int_0^2 3r\sin(\phi)\cos(\theta)|J(r,\theta,\phi)|\,\mathrm{d}r\mathrm{d}\theta\mathrm{d}\phi\\ \end{align*} \]

We need to compute the Jacobian determinant of our transformation

Example 9.2 (ctd)

Recall

\[ \begin{align*} J &= \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial \phi_1}{\partial r}\frac{\partial \phi_1}{\partial \theta}\frac{\partial \phi_1}{\partial \phi}\\ \frac{\partial \phi_2}{\partial r}\frac{\partial \phi_2}{\partial \theta}\frac{\partial \phi_2}{\partial \phi}\\ \frac{\partial \phi_3}{\partial r}\frac{\partial \phi_3}{\partial \theta}\frac{\partial \phi_3}{\partial \phi} \end{pmatrix} \\ &=\begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(r\sin(\phi)\cos(\theta)\right)&\frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}\left(r\sin(\phi)\cos(\theta)\right)&\frac{\partial}{\partial \phi}\left(r\sin(\phi)\cos(\theta)\right)\\ \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(r\sin(\phi)\sin(\theta)\right)&\frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}\left(r\sin(\phi)\sin(\theta)\right)&\frac{\partial}{\partial \phi}\left(r\sin(\phi)\sin(\theta)\right)\\ \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(r\cos(\phi)\right)&\frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}\left(r\cos(\phi)\right)&\frac{\partial}{\partial \phi}\left(r\cos(\phi)\right) \end{pmatrix} \\ &=\begin{pmatrix} \sin\phi\cos\theta&-r\sin\phi\sin\theta&r\cos\phi\cos\theta\\ \sin\phi\sin\theta&r\sin\phi\cos\theta&r\cos\phi\sin\theta\\\cos\phi&0&-r\sin\phi \end{pmatrix} \end{align*} \]

The determinant of which is given by

\[ \color{red}{\boxed{\color{white}{ |J| = r^2\sin\phi}}} \]

Example 9.2 (ctd)

Thus

\[ \int_0^{\pi/2}\int_0^{\pi/2}\int_0^2 3r\sin(\phi)\cos(\theta)|J(r,\theta,\phi)|\,\mathrm{d}r\mathrm{d}\theta\mathrm{d}\phi = \int_0^{\pi/2}\int_0^{\pi/2}\int_0^2 3r^3\sin^2(\phi)\cos(\theta)\,\mathrm{d}r\mathrm{d}\theta\mathrm{d}\phi \]

Computing the above integral gives

\[ \begin{align*} \int_0^{\pi/2}\int_0^{\pi/2}\int_0^2 3r^3\sin^2(\phi)\cos(\theta)\,\mathrm{d}r\mathrm{d}\theta\mathrm{d}\phi &= \int_0^{\pi/2}\int_0^{\pi/2}\bigg[\frac{3}{4}r^4\sin^2\phi\cos\theta\bigg]_0^2\,\mathrm{d}\theta\mathrm{d}\phi\\ &= \int_0^{\pi/2}\int_0^{\pi/2}12\sin^2\phi\cos\theta\,\mathrm{d}\theta\mathrm{d}\phi\\ &= \int_0^{\pi/2}\bigg[12\sin^2\phi\sin\theta\bigg]_0^{\pi/2}\,\mathrm{d}\phi\\ &=\int_0^{\pi/2}12\sin^2\theta\,\mathrm{d}\theta \\ &=12\bigg[\frac{\phi}{2}-\frac{\sin(2\phi)}{4}\bigg]_0^{\pi/2} = 12\left(\frac{\pi}{4}\right) = \color{red}{\boxed{\color{white}{3\pi}}} \end{align*} \]

Convergence of Fourier series

So far we have assumed convergence of the FS:

\[ f(x) \sim \frac{a_0}{2}+\sum_{n=1}^\infty \left(a_n\cos(nx)+b_n\sin(nx)\right) \]

Under these assumptions we found that the coefficients are given by the Euler integrals

\[ \color{red}{\boxed{\color{white}{ a_n = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^\pi f(x)\cos(nx)\mathrm{d}x~ \text{ and }~ b_n = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^\pi f(x)\sin(nx)\mathrm{d}x }}} \]

An important question is: for the above $a_n, b_n$ for what $x\in\mathbb{R}$ does the FS converge?

Example 5.1

Compute the Fourier series of the function

\[ f(x) = \begin{cases}x+\pi&x\in(0,\pi)\\x-\pi& x\in(-\pi,0)\end{cases} \]

such that $f(x) = f(x+2\pi)$ outside $(-\pi,\pi)$


Solution

Since $f$ is odd we have that  $\color{red}{\boxed{\color{white}{\displaystyle a_0=a_n=0}}}$

\[ \begin{align*} b_n=\frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^\pi f(x)\sin(nx)\mathrm{d}x&=\frac{2}{\pi}\int_0^\pi f(x)\sin(nx)\mathrm{d}x\\ &=\frac{2}{\pi}\bigg[-\frac{1}{n}(x+\pi)\cos(nx)\bigg]_0^\pi \\&+\frac{2}{n\pi}\int_0^\pi \cos(nx)\mathrm{d}x\\ &=\frac{2}{\pi}\bigg[-\frac{2\pi}{n}(-1)^n+\frac{\pi}{n}\bigg]\\ &= \color{#00FF00}{\boxed{\color{white}{\frac{2}{n}\left(1-2(-1)^n\right)}}} \end{align*} \]

The Fourier series of our example is given by

\[ \color{red}{\boxed{\color{white}{ f(x)\sim \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{2}{n}\left(1-2(-1)^n\right) \sin(nx) }}} \]

Recall that

\[ \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{2}{n}\left(1-2(-1)^n\right) \sin(nx) \]

means $\displaystyle \lim_{n\to\infty} s_n(x)$ where

\[ s_n(x) = \sum_{m=1}^n \frac{2}{m}\left(1-2(-1)^m\right) \sin(mx) \]

The question therefore is whether $s_n(x)$ converges for each $x$ and if it does is the limit $f(x)$?

Note

  • For the previous example it appears that the FS converges except at discontinuities

  • At points of discontinuity they converge to zero (in our example)

  • The overshoot observed at points of discontinuity is known as Gibbs phenomenon

  • A similar result holds for most functions appearing in applications

Technical preliminaries

Definition 5.1

We say that the right-hand limit of $f$ at $c$, if it exists, is given by

\[ f(c_+) = \lim_{\substack{h\to 0\\h>0}} f(c+h) \]

Similarly, the left-hand limit of $f$ at $c$ is

\[ f(c_-) = \lim_{\substack{h\to 0\\h>0}} f(c-h) \]

if it exists.

What are the left- and right-hand limts at the points $x=1, x=5$ and $x=6$?

Definition 5.2

The function $f$ is piecewise continuous on an interval $(a,b)$ if we can divide $(a,b)$ into a finite number of subintervals, on each of which $f$ is defined and continuous, and the left- and right-hand limits at the end points of each subinterval exist.

Example


  • The function consists of 4 subintervals
  • The left- and right-hand limits exist
  • The function is cts on each subinterval
  • Therefore it is PWC

Theorem 5.1 (FS convergence theorem)

Let $f$ be a periodic function with period $2\pi$ and $f$ and $f'$ piecewise continuous on $(-\pi,\pi)$. Then the FS of $f$ at $x$ converges to the value

\[ \frac{1}{2}\bigg[f(x_+) + f(x_-)\bigg] \]

i.e.

\[ \frac{1}{2}\bigg[f(x_+) + f(x_-)\bigg] = \frac{a_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\cos(nx)+b_n\sin(nx) \]

If the left- and right-hand limits are equal at $x$ then the FS equals $f(x)$

Example 5.2

Find the Fourier coefficients of the function

\[ f(x) = \begin{cases}-k&x\in(-\pi,0)\\k& x\in(0,\pi)\end{cases} \]

such that $\displaystyle f(x+2\pi) = f(x)$

Solution

Since $f$ is odd we have $\displaystyle \color{red}{\boxed{\color{white}{a_0=a_n=0}}}$

To compute $b_n$ we solve the integral

\[ b_n = \frac{2}{\pi}\int_0^\pi k\sin(nx)\mathrm{d}x = \color{red}{\boxed{\color{white}{\frac{2k}{n\pi}\left(1-(-1)^n\right)}}} \]

Example 5.3

Given that the FS of

\[ f(x) = x^2,\quad x\in(\pi,\pi) \]

is

\[ f(x) = \frac{\pi^2}{3} + \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{4}{n^2}(-1)^n\cos(nx) \]

Show that

\[ 1-\frac{1}{4}+\frac{1}{9}-\frac{1}{16}+\cdots = \frac{1}{12}\pi^2 \]

Solution

Since the function $f$ is continuous it equals its FS everywhere, so in particular

\[ f(0) = 0 = \frac{\pi^2}{3} + \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{4}{n^2}(-1)^n \]

Rearranging gives

\[ \frac{\pi^2}{3} = -4\left(-1+\frac{1}{4}-\frac{1}{9}+\frac{1}{16}-\cdots\right) \]

or

\[ \frac{\pi^2}{12} = 1-\frac{1}{4}+\frac{1}{9}-\frac{1}{16}+\cdots\quad \text{as required} \]
